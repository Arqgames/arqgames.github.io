537 BCE (or 538 BCE)
End of the Babylonian Exile and Chronological Anchor

One of the most important chronological anchors in biblical dating is the end of the Babylonian Exile. Jeremiah 25:11–12 and 29:10 explicitly state that Judah would serve Babylon for seventy years. This period is linked in 2 Chronicles 36:20–23 to the return under the decree of Cyrus, king of Persia. Daniel 9:2 shows that this seventy years was understood as a fulfillment of prophecy, and Ezra 1:1–4 records the return order in Cyrus’s first year. The mutual testimony of these books makes the end of the exile a pivotal event for biblical chronology, even within the internal scriptural framework.

The 537 BCE View

However, the absolute date is debated. The 537 BCE view takes the seventy years as a literal period, starting with the complete desolation of Jerusalem in 607 BCE. In this view, the seventy years end with the beginning of the return, i.e., 537 BCE.

Mainstream Historical View

By contrast, mainstream history and biblical scholarship date the fall of Jerusalem to 587/586 BCE, the conquest of Babylon by Cyrus to 539 BCE, and the return decree to 538 BCE. The seventy years are often understood as a symbolic or theological period representing the entire span of Babylonian dominance, rather than a strict chronological interval. These dates are anchored by ancient sources such as the Babylonian Chronicle, the Nabonidus Cylinder, and the astronomical clay tablet VAT4956.

Issues in Chronological Calculation

Thus, while the end of the exile is a crucial chronological anchor, its conversion to the Gregorian calendar depends on interpretive methods and the evaluation of extra-biblical sources.
